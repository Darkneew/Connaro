Trump won't self-quarantine

Australia and chinese students

The coronavirus has hit Australian universities hard—even if only one student, a Chinese student at the University of Queensland, has been identified as having the virus.

The University of Melbourne has offered Chinese students AU$7,500 (about $4,900) to help them circumvent travel restrictions. Like the United States, Australia is now denying entry to any foreign national who has been in China during the previous 14 days. To get around this rule, Chinese students can travel to Thailand, spend two weeks partying on a tropical vacation, then fly to Australia.

North Korea isn't ready for Coronavirus

North Korea claims it has no cases of coronavirus, according to its propaganda channels. The Pyongyang office of the World Health Organization said they received no report of an infection.

But it is unlikely that North Korea is free of COVID-19. North Korea’s rudimentary public health system simply lacks the ability to contain a major outbreak. It may reflect North Korea’s inability to test for the disease rather than the true absence of it. Testing for the virus requires specific primers and relatively sophisticated labs—both of which North Korea likely lacks.

Religion spread coronavirus in South Korea

South Korea has been preparing for a potential new strain of coronavirus since as early as November 2019. For the first four weeks of the outbreak, South Korea contained the coronavirus.

However, patient No. 31, discovered on Feb. 18, was a member of a quasi-Christian cult called Shincheonji. Although Patient No. 31 ran a high fever, she attended two Shincheonji services which held more than a thousand worshippers each. Since the discovery of Patient No. 31, the number of COVID-19 cases in South Korea jumped from 30 to 977 in eight days.

Taiwan excluded by the WHO

Taiwan, with its health care system ranked No. 1 for two years in a row in the Health Care Index. However, it was excluded from the WHO emergency meetings on the new coronavirus crisis.

Moreover, data and other related references released by WHO deliberately list coronavirus cases reported by the Taiwanese authorities as under China, regardless of the fact that Taiwan has never been under the jurisdiction of the People’s Republic of China. Taiwan, despite confirming only 34 cases as of Feb. 28, is thus listed as “very high risk” by WHO.
